How Billie Eilish’s “What was I made for?” Became the Anthem of a Generation

<-- Playlist & Listening Guide                                                                                  End -->

"I don't know how to feel, but I wanna try." – Billie Eilish

When I first watched the 2023 Barbie movie by Greta Gerwig, I wasn’t prepared for the great emotional depth that the film possessed. As Billie Eilish’s "What was I made for?" played over flashes of girlhood, growing up, and womanhood, tears quickly welled up in my eyes, and I quietly teared up. I wasn’t alone. Around me, I saw my friends and fellow audience members gently dabbing at their eyes, sniffling.

Later that night, as I casually scrolled through my social media platforms, I was met with numerous edits related to Barbie. My feed with filled with one montage after another, all linked together with the same melancholic voice of Billie Eilish that resounded through my head. These videos contained childhood firsts, teenage recklessness, getting ready with friends, experiencing love, crying through heartbreaks, empowering monologues from female figures, and so much more. I realized that my sudden moment of resonation and outbreak of emotion wasn’t personal but collective. 

As my social media pages remained occupied by “What was I made for?” along with the numerous videos set against it, it became clear that this song wasn’t a mere part of the Barbie soundtrack anymore. It had become a voice for millions of digital memories and a space for connection. Through the user-generated content on digital platforms, the song has transformed into a collaborative work that was shaped by the interpretations and reimagination of its listeners. 

The Spark of Digital Trends

“What was I made for?” quickly asserted its role as one of the top trends following the release of the Barbie movie across various digital platforms such as TikTok and Instagram. 

Some of the biggest trends include:

Montage of Girlhood:

Clips and photo compilations of the ups and downs of the creators’ own experiences of girlhood, depicting experiences highly relatable to many individuals across the world. This trend greatly emulates the montage scene in the Barbie movie. 

Example: Below are images taken of a series of photos that TikTok creator @ioannnix posted, depicting her interpretation and experiences with girlhood (@ioannnix 2023).

Childhood Nostalgia:

People posted home videos and comparisons between past and present, showing their journey from infancy to adulthood – baby steps, birthday parties, and much more – accompanied by Eilish’s soft vocals.

Example: Below are images taken of a video posted by TikTok creator @pierrettex, who put together a video comparing items from her childhood and their “adult” comparisons. She demonstrates characteristics of her girlhood while also showing growth. For instance, the two images below show the transition from toy makeup used as a young child to adult beauty products (@pierrettex 2023).

Exploring Feminism through Pop Culture:

Iconic scenes from various films such as Little Women and speeches from female public figures were compiled and remixed with "What was I made for?", highlighting significant feminist figures and the broader feminist narrative.

Example: Below are images taken of a video posted by TikTok creator @editzxmolls, who compiled scenes from the movies Little Women and Barbie and speeches by Emma Watson and Taylor Swift speaking on topics of feminism (@editzxmolls 2024).

Each trend is different, but they all revolve around the central ideas of identity, empathy, growth, and empowerment.

More Than Just a Trend

As seen through these examples, this digital activity was far from fleeting. Much of the content garnered millions of interactions. The comments flooded in agreement, and numerous viewers shared their own experiences. One viewer reflected, “I remember at 8, my teacher wanted 'strong boys' to carry the chairs. I pick[ed] up 3 to show I was just as strong, then got called a 'showoff'.” I tell people this story when they ask why [I am] a feminist.” 

Another viewer simply commented, “dam[n] why is it so accurate?” in response to @pierrettex’s nostalgic comparison video.

It’s often easy to dismiss social media as shallow, distracting, or fleeting, but the cultural phenomenon that is this song tells a different story. "What was I made for?" created a space for vulnerability. In today’s digital world, publicly released content can often feel curated, as though filtered and distant. Through this film and song, the audience becomes a part of the content rather than being a mere consumer. This shift demonstrates the creation of raw, real, and deeply emotional connections. This trend was not about going viral, but rather sharing experiences and cultivating community. 

It was as though asking, “Here’s my story – is it yours too?”

Unsurprisingly, the answer has been an overwhelming yes. 

Through Billie Eilish’s song, the user-generated content became a mirror reflecting a generation’s collective uncertainty, hope, and resilience.

A Living Song

Traditionally, when we discuss songs and films, we tend to focus on the creator’s intentions. We ask questions like, what story did Billie Eilish intend to tell? What was Greta Gerwig’s vision for Barbie?

However, “What was I made for?” turns the microphone towards its audience. While Eilish may have projected her own struggles of navigating identity and fame through her song, her listeners hear echoes of their own stories. Through the greatly personal and interpretative nature of the song’s trend, its cultural importance lies less in what was intended. Instead, the spotlight is placed on how the song was received.

This song reminds us that artistic meaning is ever-changing rather than being tied to its significance upon release. Through the modern digitized society, Eilish’s voice ties numerous personal narratives across the world. Seeing the TikTok edits, grainy childhood videos, and comments of encouragement and connection, the song now speaks for its audience. 

In this sense, “What was I made for?” is almost like that of an unfinished artwork. Rather than a static piece, its meaning is constantly morphed and reinforced as it sounds through the ears of one to another. I guess you could say that we’re all co-writers. Through this collaborative process, we directly witness how identity and feminism are actively shaped through digital culture.

It’s not just Eilish’s song anymore. It’s all of ours.
